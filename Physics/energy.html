Introduction to kinetic, potential and mechanical energy

×

  1. Kinetic Energy

    In physics, the kinetic energy of an object is the form of energy that it possesses due to its motion. It is calculated using the formula:
    KE = 1/2MV2
    Where, KE stands for Kinetic Energy.
    M stands for mass.
    V stands for velocity.
    Hence, a stationary object (V = 0) does not possess kinetic energy.

  2. Potential Energy

    In physics, potential energy is the energy held by an object because of its position relative to other objects. For Example,
    Gravitational potential Energy, which is the energy an object possess above ground, due to gravity.

    Magnetic potential energy, which is the energy two magnets posses when a south pole of one magnet can approach the north pole of another. The magnetic fields are aligned. This makes an attractive force between the two magnets creating magnetic potential energy.

    Electric potential energy, which is the energy two charged objects attracted to each other possess when being pulled apart.

    However, for this section we only going to focus on gravitational potential energy. which can be represented as following:
    PE = mgh
    Where, PE stands for Potential Energy.
    M stands for mass.
    h stands for height or distance.
    g stands for gravitational force.

  3. Mechanical energy

    (kinetic energy or potential energy) is the energy of either an object in motion or the energy that is stored in objects by their position.
    ME = KE + PE

Elastic potential energy

Elastic potential energy is the potential energy stored by stretching or compressing an elastic object by an external force such as the stretching of a spring.

Law of conservation of energy or First Law of thermodynamics

The law of conservation of energy states that energy can neither be created nor destroyed - only converted from one form of energy to another.

What is energy, work and power

×

  1. Energy is the ability to do work.

  2. Work

    is the force applied over certain displacement (W = F.d). However, we prefer including cos θ in your equation because it is very useful, especially when calculating work done on inclined planes. Therefore, we prefer equation W = F.d. Work is measured in Joules, meaning Joules is the same as Newtons (a unit of force) x Meters (a unit of displacement).

  3. Power

    is the work done over a period of time, P = F.d/t. Power is measured in Watts, meaning Watts is the same as (Newtons x meters/seconds). The more work done over less period of time, the more power.

Work-Energy Theorem: (Net Work = final Kinetic Energy - initial Kinetic Energy)

The work-energy theorem states that the net work done by the forces on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Conservative vs Nonconservative forces

×

    Conservative vs Nonconservative forces

  1. Conservative Forces

    Forces independant to the path taken.
    For example:
    Elastic forces
    Electric forces
    Gravity

  3. Nonconservative Forces

    Forces dependant to the path taken.
    For example:
    Applied force
    Frictional force
  4. Net workdone = Workdone by Conservative Forces + Workdone by Nonconservative forces (Wnet = Wcf + Wncf)
    Net Workdone = ΔKe
  5. Workdone by conservative forces is equal to negative change in Potential energy
    Wcf = - ΔPe
  6. Workdone by nonconservative forces is equal to Network done - work done by conservative forces
    Wncf = Wnet - Wcf
  7. Hence, Wncf = ΔKe - (-ΔPe)
    Wncf = ΔKe + ΔPe
    Me = Ke + Pe
    ΔMe = ΔKe + ΔPe
    Therefore, ΔMe = Wncf
