What is Current

A Number of electrons that passes a certain point in a circuit per second and is measured in Amps.

As said before 1 coulombs = 6.25 x 10^18 electrons, therefore 1 amp = 1 Coulumbs per second (1C/s), 3 Amps = 3 Coulumbs per second (3C/s)

Electric conductor

For current to flow, an electric conductor is needed, something that allows the flow of charge (electric current) in one or more directions through the movement of electrons in one direction or back and forth.

Insulator

Something that does not allow movement or exchange of electrons.

What is a Resistor

Resistors are components in an electric circuit used to reduce current flow and are measured in ohms.

Introduction to Ohm's law (circuits)

×

Ohm's law

Ohm's law states that the current through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the voltage and inversely proportional to the resistance across two points.

And can be mathematically represented as follows:

I = V/R

Where:

  • I is Current of the system
  • V is Voltage of the system
  • R is total resistance of the system

This Hence, represents the basic relationship between these three important components of an electric system or circuit.

The current is therefore highly dependent on voltage and resistance, for example, in general increasing the voltage should increase the current, and increasing the resistance should decrease the current.

Looking at this in terms of forces, voltage would the applied force, Current would the velocity and resistance would be the frictional force.

The effects of connecting resistors in series

When resistors are connected in series, all resistors experience the same current but the voltage is divided amongst all resistors, e.g:

Four resistors are connected in series:

  • R1 = 2 ohms
  • R2 = 4 ohms
  • R3 = 6 ohms
  • R4 = 8 ohms
  • V of the system = 120 volts

To find how much each resistor reduces voltage, one first needs to find the total current of the circuit by finding the sum of all resistors, Totalresistance = R1 + R2 + R3 + R4
= 2 ohms + 4 ohms + 6 ohms + 8 ohms
= 20 hms.
Then find the current by:
I = V/R
= 120 V/20 ohms
= 6 Amps.

All resistors will experience a current of 6 Amps, now you can find the voltage reduction of each resistor using the current you just calculated (6 Amps) multiplied by the resistance of each resistor.

Therefore, Voltage reduction at R1 is equal to:
V = I x R = 6 Amps x 2 ohms
= 12 volts.
Hence, if you placed a voltmeter after R1, you'd get a reading of 108 volts.

Voltage reduction at R2 is equal to:
V = I x R = 6 Amps x 4 ohms
= 24 volts. Hence, R2 will experience a voltage of 108 volts and reduce it by 24 volts and if you placed a voltmeter after R2, you'd get a reading of 84 volts.

Voltage reduction at R3 is equal to:
V = I x R = 6 Amps x 6 ohms
= 36 volts. Hence, R3 will experience a voltage of 84 volts and reduce it by 36 volts and if you placed a voltmeter after R3, you'd get a reading of 48 volts.

Voltage reduction at R4 is equal to:
V = I x R = 6 Amps x 8 ohms
= 48 volts. Hence, R4 will experience a voltage of 48 volts and reduce it by 48 volts and if you placed a voltmeter after R4, you'd get a reading of 0 volts.

Thus, the sum of the voltage reductions should be equal to the voltage of the power source, e.g. The battery.

The effects of connecting resistors in parallel

When resistors are connected in parallel, all resistors experience the same voltage but the current is divided amongst all resistors, e.g:

Four resistors are connected in parallel:

  • R1 = 2 ohms
  • R2 = 4 ohms
  • R3 = 6 ohms
  • R4 = 8 ohms
  • V of the system = 120 volts

To find how much each resistor reduces current,

Therefore Current at R1 is equal to:
I1 = V/R
= 120 V/2 ohms
= 60 Amps.
Hence, if you placed an ammeter after R1, you'd get a reading of 60 Amps

Current at R2 is equal to:
I2 = V/R
= 120 V/4 ohms
= 40 Amps.
Hence, if you placed a ammeter after R2, you'd get a reading of 40 Amps

Current at R3 is equal to: I3 = V/R
= 120 V/6 ohms
= 20 Amps. Hence, if you placed a ammeter after R3, you'd get a reading of 20 Amps

Current reduction at R4 is equal to:
I4 = V/R
= 120 V/8 ohms
= 15 Amps.
Hence, if you placed a ammeter after R4, you'd get a reading of 15 Amps

Therefore, the total current of the system is 135 Amps because the sum of all resistor current readings should be equal to the total current of the circuit.

Total Resistance:
1/Rtotal = (1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + 1/R4)-1
1/Rtotal = (1/2 + 1/4 + 1/6 + 1/8)-1
Another take away, the more you add resistors in series, resistance goes up, but the more you add resistors in parallel, resistance goes down.

What is electric power

The rate at which electric energy is transferred by an electric circuit (measured in Watt) or the work done by the circuit per second, hence 1 Watt is equivalent to 1 Joules per second.

Power equations:

  • P = IV
  • P = I2 x R
  • P = V2/R
  • P = -qΔV/t

P = I2 x R and P = V2/R are both derived from P = VI.

Remember, I = V/R, therefore replace I in the Power equation with V/R. P = V/R x V, which is simplified to P = V2/R.

V = I x R, therefore replace V in the power equation with I x R. P = I x IR, which is simplified to P = I2 x R

P = -qΔV/t is derived from electrostatic Electric potential and voltage section, where W = -qΔV. power is workdone per second, therefore Power = -qΔV/t.
