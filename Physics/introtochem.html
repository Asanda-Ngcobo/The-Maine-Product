Alkali Metals

    The alkali metals are all:

    • Shiny
    • Soft
    • Highly reactive
    • Low melting points
    • Low densities (lower than other metals)
    • Low electronegativity
    • Low ionization energy
    • Good conductors of heat and electricity
    • Ductile - the physical property of the metal which means if we pull the metal it's going to stretch rather than break,
    • Malleable - The ability of a substance, usually metal, to be deformed or moulded into a different shape,

    at standard temperature and pressure and readily lose their outermost electron to form cations with charge +1.

    They can all be cut easily with a knife due to their softness, exposing a shiny surface that tarnishes rapidly in air due to oxidation by atmospheric moisture and oxygen (and in the case of lithium, nitrogen). All the alkali metals react with water, with the heavier alkali metals reacting more vigorously than the lighter ones.

    Because of their high reactivity, they must be stored under oil to prevent reaction with air, and are found naturally only in salts and never as the free elements.

    Caesium

    The fifth alkali metal, is the most reactive of all the metals.

×

Alkaline Earth Metals

    The alkaline earth metals are all:

    • Shiny
    • Hard
    • Silvery-white
    • Somewhat reactive.
    • Low melting points, but higher than that of alkali metals.
    • Low electronegativity.
    • Low ionization energy.
    • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
    • Ductile - the physical property of the metal which means if we pull the metal it's going to stretch rather than break.
    • Malleable - The ability of a substance, usually metal, to be deformed or moulded into a different shape.

    at standard temperature and pressure and readily lose their outermost two electrons to form cations with charge +2.

    All the alkaline earth metals except beryllium also react with water to form strongly alkaline hydroxides and, thus, should be handled with great care. The heavier alkaline earth metals react more vigorously than the lighter ones.

×

Transition Metals

    A transition metals are one which forms one or more stable ions which have incompletely filled d orbitals, they.

    • Usually hard and tough, at standard temperature and pressure.
    • Higher melting and boiling points,
    • Low electronegativity
    • Low ionization energy.
    • Low electron affinity.
    • High conductors of heat and electricity.
    • Ductile - the physical property of the metal which means if we pull the metal it's going to stretch rather than break.
    • Highly Malleable - The ability of a substance, usually metal, to be deformed or moulded into a different shape.



    Transition Metals are low reactive with water and oxygen, which explains why they resist corrosion.

×

Post Transition Metals

    Post Transition metals are all:

    • Soft
    • Brittle - hard but liable to break easily, opposite of Ductile and malleable
    • Lower melting and boiling points, compared to transition metals
    • Low electronegativity
    • Low ionization energy.
    • Low electron affinity.
    • Poor conductors of heat and electricity.



    Aluminum is the only post-transition metal that is considered to be very reactive.

×

Metalloids

    Metalloids can be defined as chemical elements whose physical and chemical properties fall in between the metal and non-metal categories. Boron, germanium, silicon, antimony, arsenic, tellurium and pollanium are the seven most widely recognized metalloids, they:

    • Look like metals but behave largely like nonmetals.
    • Brittle - hard but liable to break easily, opposite of Ductile and malleable
    • Shiny
    • Intermediate to relatively good conductors of heat and electricity.



    Silicon, immediately below carbon in group 4A, is by far the most abundant metalloid, being present in over 27% of the Earth's crust. Silicon forms strong bonds with oxygen.

×

Non-metals

    Non-metals are the elements which form negative ions by accepting or gaining electrons. Non-metals usually have 4, 5, 6 or 7 electrons in their outermost shell.

    • May be Solid, Liquid or gas at standard temperature and pressure.
    • Brittle - hard but liable to break easily, opposite of Ductile and malleable
    • Neither strong nor tough
    • Relatively low density
    • Higher Ionization energy
    • Higher electronegativity
    • Bad conductors of heat and electricity.



    Most nonmetals have biological, technological or domestic applications. Living organisms are composed almost entirely of the nonmetals hydrogen, oxygen, carbon, and nitrogen. Nearly all nonmetals have individual uses in medicine, pharmaceuticals, lighting, lasers, and household items.

×

Noble gases

    The noble gases also known as the inert gases or rare gases, are group 18 on the periodic table, which is the column of elements on the far right side of the table. There are seven noble gas elements: helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon, and oganesson.

    • As the their name suggest, they are all gas at standard temperature and pressure, except for oganesson. There haven't been enough atoms produced of oganesson to know its phase for certain, but most scientists predict it will be a liquid or solid.
    • Relatively low density
    • Higher Ionization energy
    • Unknown electronegativity


    Noble gases are the least reactive chemical elements. They are nearly inert because the atoms have a full valence electron shell, with little tendency to accept or donate electrons to form chemical bonds.

×

Lanthanides

    The lanthanide or lanthanoid series of chemical elements comprises of elements numbers 57- 71, from lanthanum through lutetium. These elements, along with the chemically similar elements scandium and yttrium, are often collectively known as the rare-earth elements or rare-earth metals, they are:

    • Relatively soft metals
    • High melting points and boiling points
    • Very reactive


    Although the lanthanides are sometimes called the rare earths, the elements are not particularly rare. However, they are difficult to separate from one another.

×

Actinides

    The actinide or actinoid series encompasses of elements numbers from 89 to 103, actinium through lawrencium. The actinide series derives its name from the first element in the series, actinium.

    • All are radioactive
    • The metals tarnish readily in air.
    • Actinide metals tend to be fairly soft.
    • Actinides are very dense metals with distinctive structures.
    • They react with boiling water or dilute acid to release hydrogen gas.


    All actinides are radioactive and release energy upon radioactive decay; naturally occurring uranium and thorium, and synthetically produced plutonium are the most abundant actinides on Earth. Uranium and is very important for nuclear energy production as when “enriched” into U-235 concentrations can be used as fuel for nuclear power plants and the nuclear reactors that run naval ships and submarines. The first atomic bomb ever created, subsequently used to bomb Hiroshima, was made from highly-enriched uranium-235.

Hydrogen

  1. Intro

    2. Hydrogen is the chemical element with the symbol H and atomic number 1, atomic mass 1.0079. It is the lightest element.


    It is the most abundant chemical substance in the universe, constituting roughly 75% of all normal matter.


  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

    3. It is a gas of diatomic molecules having the formula H₂.


    It is colorless, odorless, tasteless, non-toxic, and highly combustible.

  3. The most common isotope of this element

    4. For the most common isotope of hydrogen (symbol 1H) each atom has one proton, one electron, and no neutrons.


  4. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    5. Hydrogen is nonmetallic (except it becomes metallic at extremely high pressures), and readily forms a single covalent bond with most nonmetallic elements, forming compounds such as water and nearly all organic compounds.


  5. Electron Configuration

    6. 1s1

  6. Electronegativity

    7. 2.2

  7. Additional info

    8. Hydrogen plays a particularly important role in acid-base reactions because these reactions usually involve the exchange of protons between soluble molecules.

Helium

  1. Intro

    2. Helium is the chemical element with the symbol He, atomic number 2 and atomic mass 4,0026.


    It is the second lightest and second most abundant element in the observable universe, after hydrogen.


  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

    3. Its boiling point is the lowest among all the elements, and it does not have a melting point at standard pressure.


    It is a colorless, odorless, tasteless, non-toxic, inert, monatomic gas.

  3. The most common isotope of this element

    4. The most common isotope of helium in the universe is helium-4, the vast majority of which was formed during the Big Bang.


  4. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    5. Noble Gas.


  5. Electron Configuration

    6. 1s2

  6. Electronegativity

    7. Unknown

  7. Additional info

    8. Its abundance is similar to this in both the Sun and in Jupiter, due to the very high nuclear binding energy (per nucleon) of helium-4, with respect to the next three elements after helium. This helium-4 binding energy also accounts for why it is a product of both nuclear fusion and radioactive decay. Large amounts of new helium are created by nuclear fusion of hydrogen in stars.

Lithium

  1. Intro

    2. Lithium is the chemical element with the symbol Li, atomic number 3 and atomic mass 6,941.



  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

    3. It is a soft, silvery-white alkali metal. Under standard conditions, it is the least dense metal and the least dense solid element.


    Like all alkali metals, lithium is highly reactive and flammable, and must be stored in vacuum, inert atmosphere, or inert liquid such as purified kerosene or mineral oil


  3. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    4. Alkali metal


  4. Electron Configuration

    5. 1s22s1

  5. Electronegativity

    6. 0.98

  6. Additional info

    7. When cut, it exhibits a metallic luster, but moist air corrodes it quickly to a dull silvery gray, then black tarnish. It never occurs freely in nature, but only in (usually ionic) compounds, such as pegmatitic minerals, which were once the main source of lithium. Due to its solubility as an ion, it is present in ocean water and is commonly obtained from brines. Lithium metal is isolated electrolytically from a mixture of lithium chloride and potassium chloride.

    2. Beryllium is the chemical element with the symbol Be, atomic number 4 and atomic mass 9,0122.



  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

    3. It is a steel-gray, strong, lightweight and brittle. It is a divalent element that occurs naturally only in combination with other elements to form minerals.


    Notable gemstones high in beryllium include beryl (aquamarine, emerald) and chrysoberyl.


  3. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    4. Alkaline earth metal


  4. Electron Configuration

    5. 1s22s2

  5. Electronegativity

    6. 1.57

  6. Additional info

    7. It is a relatively rare element in the universe, usually occurring as a product of the spallation of larger atomic nuclei that have collided with cosmic rays. Within the cores of stars, beryllium is depleted as it is fused into heavier elements. Beryllium constitutes about 0.0004 percent by mass of Earth's crust. The world's annual beryllium production of 220 tons is usually manufactured by extraction from the mineral beryl, a difficult process because beryllium bonds strongly to oxygen.

  3. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    5. 1s22s22p4

All plants, animals, and fungus need oxygen for cellular respiration, which extracts energy by the reaction of oxygen with molecules derived from food and produces carbon dioxide as a waste product. Many major classes of organic molecules in living organisms contain oxygen atoms, such as proteins, nucleic acids, carbohydrates, and fats, as do the major constituent inorganic compounds of animal shells, teeth, and bone. Most of the mass of living organisms is oxygen as a component of water, the major constituent of lifeforms.

  1. Intro

    2. Flourine is the chemical element with the symbol F, atomic number 9 and atomic mass 18,998.



  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

    3. It is the lightest halogen and exists at standard conditions as a highly toxic, pale yellow diatomic gas. As the most electronegative reactive element, it is extremely reactive, as it reacts with all other elements except for the light inert gases.



  3. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    4. A highly reactive nonmetal


  4. Electron Configuration

    5. 1s22s22p5

  5. Electronegativity

    6. 3.98

  6. Additional info

    7. Owing to the expense of refining pure fluorine, most commercial applications use fluorine compounds, with about half of mined fluorite used in steelmaking. The rest of the fluorite is converted into corrosive hydrogen fluoride en route to various organic fluorides, or into cryolite, which plays a key role in aluminium refining. Molecules containing a carbon–fluorine bond often have very high chemical and thermal stability; their major uses are as refrigerants, electrical insulation and cookware, and PTFE (Teflon). Pharmaceuticals such as atorvastatin and fluoxetine contain C−F bonds. The fluoride ion from dissolved fluoride salts inhibits dental cavities, and so finds use in toothpaste and water fluoridation. Global fluorochemical sales amount to more than US$69 billion a year.

It is obtained industrially by mining bauxite rock, which is high in aluminium minerals.

  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

  3. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

The late 20th century to early 21st century has been described as the Silicon Age (also known as the Digital Age or Information Age) because of the large impact that elemental silicon has on the modern world economy. The small portion of very highly purified elemental silicon used in semiconductor electronics is essential to the transistors and integrated circuit chips used in most modern technology such as smartphones and other computers. In 2019, 32.4% of the semiconductor market segment was for networks and communications devices, and the semiconductors industry is projected to reach $726.73 billion by 2027.

Phosphorus

  1. Intro

    2. Phosphorus is the chemical element with the symbol P, atomic number 15 and atomic mass 30,976.



  2. State at standard pressure and pressure

    3. Elemental phosphorus exists in two major forms, white phosphorus and red phosphorus, but because it is highly reactive, phosphorus is never found as a free element on Earth.



  3. Metal, Metalloid or Non-metal

    4. Non-metal


  4. Electron Configuration

    5. 1s22s22p63s23p3

  5. Electronegativity

    6. 2.19

  6. Additional info

    7. Phosphorus is an element essential to sustaining life largely through phosphates, compounds containing the phosphate ion, PO43-. Phosphates are a component of DNA, RNA, ATP, and phospholipids, complex compounds fundamental to cells. Elemental phosphorus was first isolated from human urine, and bone ash was an important early phosphate source. Phosphate mines contain fossils because phosphate is present in the fossilized deposits of animal remains and excreta. Low phosphate levels are an important limit to growth in a number of plant ecosystems. The vast majority of phosphorus compounds mined are consumed as fertilisers. Phosphate is needed to replace the phosphorus that plants remove from the soil, and its annual demand is rising nearly twice as fast as the growth of the human population. Other applications include organophosphorus compounds in detergents, pesticides, and nerve agents.

Atoms

Are the most basic form of everything that exist in the universe. They are made of protons and neutrons found inside the nucleus, and electrons that orbits around the nucleus.


  • Protons, are positively charged particles found inside the nucleus and their number is used to represent the atomic number of every element found in the periodic table, because unlike neutrons and electrons, their number per each atom is always the same and and are represented with p+ symbol.
  • Neutrons, are particles found inside the nucleus, they have no charge, play a vital part in keeping the nucleus intact by preventing protons from repelling one another, being attracted to electrons orbiting the nucleus and they are responsible for formation of Isotopes.
  • Electrons, are negatively charged particles found outside the nucleus of an atom, they play a crutial part in chemical bonding and are represented with e- symbol

Atoms have what called electron configuration , the maximum number of electrons each shell can have on its orbit. for better understanding, visualize our solar system, the sun would be like the nucleus, the orbits of each planet would be the shells and planets would be the electrons. However, the orbits would have more than one planet. To find how many electrons each orbit can hold, use this equation:


  • e- for Shell(n) = 2n2, n represents shell number. For the second shell to have electrons, the first shell needs to be full, for the third shell to have electrons, the second shell needs to be full first

  • Using the equation above let's found how many electrons the first orbit can hold:


    e- for Shell(n) = 2n2
    e- for Shell(1) = 2(1)2
    e- for Shell(2) = 2(1)
    e- for Shell(1) = 2 e-
    Therefore, the first shell can only hold a maximum two electrons.

  • How about the second shell?

    e- for Shell(n) = 2n2
    e- for Shell(2) = 2(2)2
    e- for Shell(2) = 2(4)
    e- for Shell(2) = 8 e-
    The second shell can only hold a maximum eight electrons.

  • How about the third shell?

    e- for Shell(n) = 2n2
    e- for Shell(3) = 2(3)2
    e- for Shell(3) = 2(9)
    e- for Shell(3) = 18 e- ,
    The third shell can only hold a maximum eighteen electrons.

  • How about the forth shell?

    e- for Shell(n) = 2n2
    e- for Shell(4) = 2(4)2
    e- for Shell(4) = 2(16)
    e- for Shell(4) = 32 e-
    The third shell can only hold a maximum thirty two electrons.

  • How about the fifth shell?

    e- for Shell(n) = 2n2
    e- for Shell(5) = 2(5)2
    e- for Shell(5) = 2(25)
    e- for Shell(5) = 50 e-
    The third shell can only hold a maximum fifty electrons.

  • How about the sixth shell?

    e- for Shell(n) = 2n2
    e- for Shell(6) = 2(6)2
    e- for Shell(6) = 2(36)
    e- for Shell(6) = 72 e-
    The third shell can only hold a maximum seventhy two electrons.

Valence shell and valence electrons

×

Valence electrons and valence shell

Due to electron configuration and number of electrons, valence electrons and valence shells are formed by every element, valence electrons are electrons found in the outermost shell of element and that outermost shell is called valence shell.
e.g.

Carbon has 6 electrons in its natural state, so based on that and electron configuration of 2, 8, 18, 32, 52 and 72, carbon has two shells with first one having 2 electrons and second one with 4. Therefore, the second shell of carbon is the valence shell, with 4 valence electrons.

Phosphorus has 15 electrons in its natural state, so based on that and electron configuration of 2, 8, 18, 32, 52 and 72, Phosphorus has three shells with first one having 2 electrons, second one with 8 and third one with 5 electrons . Therefore, the third shell of Phosphorus is the valence shell, with 5 valence electrons.

So, why do we care so much about valence shells and valence electrons?

Well, they play a vital part in chemical bonding, knowing the valence shell and valence electrons can help you predict how two elements will react with each other.

Octet rule

The desire for most elements to have 8 electrons in their valence shell.

Electronegativity

×

What is electronegativity

Electronegativity is the ability for an element to attract electrons to itself from other elements, the more you go to the right of the periodic table, the higher the electronegativity gets and the higher you are on the periodic table, the higher the electronegativity compared the one below you.

Therefore, Non-metals overall have a higher electronegativity than Metals and Metalloids, and the non-metals at the top of the periodic table, have a higher electronegativity than the one below them.

E.g.

Flourine has the highest electronegativity of them all, because it is on the far-right and top of the periodic, followed by Oxygen, because oxygen is right before Flourine and on top of its group.

So,

  • Valence shell
  • Valence electrons
  • Octet rule
  • Electronegativity
are four of the element's properties that plays a crucial role in chemical bonding:

If the element's valence shell is the second shell and have three or less valence electrons, that element have a low electronegativity and will not try to achieve the octet rule, instead it will try to get rid of its second valence shell and become like helium.

If the element's valence shell is equal to or greater to second shell, the element would try to achieve the octet rule by either gaining or losing electrons on its valence shell.

Chemical bonds- Intramolecular forces (Ionic, and Covelant bonds)

×

Chemical bonding

Chemical bonding occurs due to the desire to have a full valence shell. The ones with 5 or less overall electron in their naturak state would try to have their first shell as their valence shell by losing electrons and one with 6 or more overall electrons, (from Oxygen upward) in natural state will try to achieve the octet rule by either losing or gaining electrons.


Ionic Chemical

Involves the transfer of electrons from low electronegativity, three or less valence electrons elements to high electronegativity, four or more valence electrons elements. Hence, usual from metals to non-metals. The electronegativity difference is usual greater 1.70 units.

E.g.
Beryllium oxide (Be2+O2-), Beryllium has 4 overall electrons on its natural state, two shells (first shell has two electrons and the second one has two electrons (valence electrons)), low electronegativity of 0.57 units. Beryllium therefore desires the helium state.


Oxygen on the other hand has 8 overall electrons on its natural state, two shells (first shell has two electrons and the second one with 6 ), high electronegativity of 3.44 units. Oxygen therefore desires the octet state.


Thus, when they close to each other, Beryllium will give away its two electrons to oxygen, and beryllium will only left with one filled first shell with two electrons, achieving the helium state with 2 valence electrons (with the first shell becoming its valence shell).


Oxygen will gain the two electrons, achieving the octet state with 8 valence electrons.

By the way, the electronegativity between Oxygen and Beryllium is 2.87 units

Covalent bonding

Involves the sharing of electrons between elements with high electronegativity. . The electronegativity difference is usual between 0.00 and 1.70 units.

Covalent bonding is more common in organim chemistry, hence in living things such as humans, animals, trees and microorganisms.

London Dispersion Forces

Are formed by atoms at their natural state, as the electrons orbits the nucleus, these electrons sometimes they find themselves all or most of them on the left-side of thier atom atom making that side partially negative and the right-side partially positive and vice versa.


So if there is a close by atom on the left, that atom's electrons will repelled to the left also (Due to the like-charges repel effect), causing that close by atom's right side to be partially positive, causing an attraction between these two atoms (Due to the opposite-charges attract effect). This can therefore cause a domino effect throughout all close by atoms.

This force is sometimes called an induced dipole-induced dipole attraction or temporal dipole attraction.

Partial charge is represented by greek delta and the charge:

  • Partial negative = δ-
  • Partial positive = δ+

Bond Polarity

It is when a molecule has two different charges on its opposite sides, in covalent bonded molecules, this happens when the sharing of electrons is highly uneven due to too much difference in their electronegativity.

  • Polar covelant bonds : The electronegativity difference is between 0.41 and 1.70 units. The closer the difference to 1.70, the higher polarity.

On the other hand, all ionic bonded molecules are polar, since one atom gives away its electrons and becomes positively due to more number of protons compared to electrons, and the one that receives the electron/s becomes negatively charged due to now having more electrons than protons.

VSEPR theory

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion theory is a model used to predict 3-D molecular geometry or shapesbased on the number of valence shell electron bond pairs among the atoms in a molecule or ion


Linear shape (180°)

When the central atom has zero lone pairs and the surrounded by two atoms.


Trigonal-planar shape(120°)

When the central atom has zero lone pairs and the surrounded by three atoms.


Bent shape (116°)

When the central atom has one lone pair and the surrounded by two atoms.


Tetrahedral shape (109.5°)

When the central atom has zero lone pairs and the surrounded by four atoms.


Trigonal-pyramidal shape (less than 109.5°)

When the central atom has one lone pair and the surrounded by three atoms.
