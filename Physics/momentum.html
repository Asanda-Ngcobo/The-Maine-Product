Introduction to Momentum

Momentum is the quantity of motion of a moving body, measured as a product of its mass and velocity. measured in kg.m/s-1

Momentum and Impulse

Impulse is the integral of a force, F, over the time interval, t, for which it acts. Since force is a vector quantity, impulse is also a vector quantity.

Conservation of Momentum

The law of conservation of momentum is derived for newton's third and second law.

    Conservation of Momentum

  1. Newton's third law states that, if object A exerts force on object B, Object B also exerts an equal force on an opposite direction
  2. FA = -FB
  3. Newton's second law states, Force is equal to mass multiplied by acceleration (F = m.a). Therefore,
  4. mA.aA = -mB.aB
  5. We can then also expand acceleration, which is equal to final velocity - intial velocity divided by change in time (vf - vi/ Δt )
  6. m(vf - vi/ Δt ) = -m(vf - vi/ Δt ), Multiply by change in time on both sides will cancel them and then will be left with the Equation:
  7. mA(vf - vi)A = -mB(vf - vi)B, multiply final velocity and initial velocity of Object B only by the negative, not mass because mass is scalar
  8. mA(vf - vi)A = mB(- vf + vi)B, multiply the mass with the velocities on both sides.
  9. mAvf - mAvi = -mBvf + mBvi, put initial momentum of A and B on the same side, and final momemntum on the same side.
  10. mAvf + mBvf = mAvi + mBvi

Elastic vs Inelatic collision

An elastic collision is a collision in which there is no net loss in kinetic energy in the system as a result of the collision and an Inelastic collisioni is a collision in which there is a loss of kinetic energy. While momentum of the system is conserved in an inelastic collision, kinetic energy is not.
