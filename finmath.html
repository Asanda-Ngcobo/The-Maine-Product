    Introduction to Financial Maths

    Financial Maths deals with calculations of monetary value.

  1. Monetary value

    Is the value of something measured in terms of money, and the monetary value can change based on the following factors:

    • Interest

      Is the monetary charge for borrowing or saving money, generally expressed as a percentage, such as an annual percentage rate (APR).

    • Inflation

      A general increase in prices and fall in the purchasing value of money.

    • Appreciation

      an increase in the value of an asset over time.

    • Depreciation

      a decrease in the value of an asset over time.

  2. Rate of Change

    r is the percentage (%) at which monetary value change and i is the monetary value change in actual money.
    E.g. if you borrowed $5000 and annual interest rate is 8%. r is therefore 8% and i is $400 (8% of $5000c) every 12 months. i can be used to represent appreciation, depreciation, interest and inflation.

  3. i can either be simple or compounded


    • Simple i

      i is applicable to the initial amount forever, e.g. if you put $1000 in your savings account and the bank gives 10% yearly interest (r), the bank will therefore give you $100 every year (i). Hence, in five years you will have $1500.

    • Compound i

      i is applicable to the change of the initial amount, e.g. if you put $1000 in your savings account and the bank gives 10% yearly interest, the bank will therefore give you $100 every year(10% of 1000), making your balance to $1100.
      $110 the second year (10% of 1100), making your balance $1210.
      $121 the third year (10% of $1331), etc.
      Hence, in five years you will have $1607,51.

Simple interest

×
  1. The equation for simple interest is I = Prt, where:

    • I

      is the Interest earned or payed.

    • P

      is the Principal, the initial amount deposited or borrowed.

    • r

      is the rate in percentage, earned or charged from the principal each passing time, which is usually per year for simple interest.

    • t

      is the amount of time, the principal is going to stay inside your account or the time it is going to take to back the principal borrowed.
      e.g. You deposit $2000 and the bank gives you a annual interest rate of 0.5%.
      This means if your $2000 remains in your account for 4 years, your account will have $2400.
    This equation is used to calculate the interest in money earned over time.
  2. Calculating the Final amount, A = P(1 + rt) where:

    • A

      is the Final amount you will earn or pay after a given time.

    • P

      is the Principal, the initial amount deposited or borrowed.

    • r

      is the rate, the percentage you earn or charged from the Principal for a given time, which is usually per year for simple interest .

    • t

      is the amount of time, the principal is going to stay inside your account or the time it is going to take to back the principal borrowed.
  3. A = P (1 + rt) is actually derived from A = P + I.
    Meaning final amount earned or payed (A) is equal to the amount deposited or borrowed plus the interest earned or payed over a given time.
    • A = P + I
    • Since I = Prt, I is substitated by Prt in the A = P + I formula and get A = P + Prt.
    • Then P is factored out, to get a final equation of A = P(1 + rt).

  1. Compound Interest math problems

    Interest is usually compounded yearly, semi-yearly, quartely, or monthly.

    • Yearly

      means once a year, therefore n = 1

    • Semi-yearly

      means twice a year, therefore n = 2

    • Quartely

      Four times a year, therefore n = 4

    • Monthly

      12 times a year, therefore n = 12

    • Continuously

      there is no limit to how often interest can compound. Compounding continuously can occur an infinite number of times, meaning a balance is earning interest at all times

  2. Equation for interest compounded by any format except continuously.

    A = P(1 + r/n)nt
    • A = final amount
    • P = initial amount
    • r = interest rate
    • n = number of times interest applied per time period (yearly, semi-yearly, quartely, monthly, etc.)
    • t = number of years
